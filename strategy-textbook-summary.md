## PART 1: CONCEPTS AND TECHNIQUES (12 Chapters)

### Section A: Introduction and Overview

#### Chapter 1: What Is Strategy and Why Is It Important?
- **Strategy defined:** Management's game plan to stake out a market position, attract and please customers, conduct operations, compete successfully, and achieve performance targets
- **Business model:** Management's storyline for how the company will generate revenue and profit — must have a demand model and cost model
- **Winning strategy** fits company's external situation + internal resources, builds competitive advantage, and boosts performance
- **Core management functions:** Crafting and executing strategy are THE core top management functions
- **5-Phase Managerial Process:**
  1. Develop a strategic vision (long-term direction)
  2. Set objectives (financial + strategic goals; Balanced Scorecard)
  3. Craft strategy (responses to environment, competitive moves, capabilities)
  4. Implement & execute (operations-oriented, make-things-happen)
  5. Evaluate & adjust (corrective actions based on actual results)
- **Strategy is collaborative:** At EDS, 2,500 employees involved; at J.M. Smucker, 140 employees spent 25% time over 6 months
- **Levels of strategy:**
  - Single-business: 3 levels (business → functional → operating)
  - Diversified: 4 levels (corporate → business → functional → operating)

#### Chapter 2: Charting a Company's Direction — Vision, Mission, Objectives, Strategy
- **Mission** = "who we are, what we do, why we're here" (current purpose)
- **Vision** = "what we will become" (future business scope and aspirations)
- **Core values** guide the pursuit of the mission
- **Strategic plan** = vision + objectives + strategy
- **Strategic intent** = unwavering commitment to execute the plan
- **Board of Directors' 4 roles:**
  1. Critically appraise strategic plan and execution
  2. Evaluate caliber of senior executives' strategic leadership
  3. Institute compensation tied to stakeholder interests
  4. Ensure accurate financial reporting and controls

### Section B: Core Concepts and Analytical Tools

#### Chapter 3: Evaluating a Company's External Environment
**7-Question External Analysis Framework:**

| # | Question | Key Tools |
|---|----------|-----------|
| 1 | What are the industry's dominant economic features? | Market size, growth, scope, differentiation, scale economies |
| 2 | How strong are competitive forces? | **Porter's Five Forces** |
| 3 | What forces are driving industry change? | Driving forces analysis |
| 4 | What market positions do rivals occupy? | **Strategic group mapping** |
| 5 | What strategic moves will rivals make? | Competitor analysis, game theory |
| 6 | What are the key success factors (KSFs)? | KSF identification |
| 7 | Is the industry outlook attractive? | Industry attractiveness assessment |

**Porter's Five Forces:**
1. Rivalry among existing competitors
2. Threat of substitutes
3. Threat of new entrants
4. Bargaining power of suppliers
5. Bargaining power of buyers

**Common Driving Forces:** Industry growth rate changes, globalization, internet capabilities, buyer composition shifts, product/process innovation, marketing innovation, entry/exit of major firms, tech diffusion, regulatory changes, societal shifts

**Strategic Group Mapping:** Rivals in same/nearby strategic groups = close competitors. Distant groups = little threat.

#### Chapter 4: Evaluating a Company's Resources, Capabilities, and Competitiveness
**5-Question Internal Analysis Framework:**

| # | Question | Key Tools |
|---|----------|-----------|
| 1 | How well is the present strategy working? | Qualitative + quantitative assessment |
| 2 | What are strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, threats? | **SWOT Analysis** |
| 3 | Are prices and costs competitive? | **Value chain analysis**, benchmarking |
| 4 | Is the company stronger or weaker than rivals? | Competitive strength assessment |
| 5 | What issues merit front-burner attention? | Strategic "worry list" |

**Value Chain Analysis:** How competently a company manages activities relative to rivals → building blocks for competitive advantage

**SWOT Output:** Translate diagnosis into actions → improve strategy → improve business prospects

### Section C: Crafting a Strategy

#### Chapter 5: The Five Generic Competitive Strategies

```
                    PRODUCT/PROCESS FOCUS
                    LOW COST        DIFFERENTIATION
TARGET  ┌──────────┬─────────────┬──────────────────┐
MARKET  │ BROAD    │ 1. Overall  │ 2. Broad         │
        │          │ Low-Cost    │ Differentiation  │
        │          │ Provider    │                  │
        │          ├─────────────┼──────────────────┤
        │          │      3. Best-Cost Provider     │
        │          │     (Hybrid: Value for Money)  │
        ├──────────┼─────────────┼──────────────────┤
        │ NARROW   │ 4. Focused  │ 5. Focused       │
        │ (Niche)  │ Low-Cost    │ Differentiation  │
        └──────────┴─────────────┴──────────────────┘
```

**1. Overall Low-Cost Provider:**
- Meaningfully lower costs than rivals (not necessarily absolute lowest)
- Works when: products are identical/weakly differentiated, many price-sensitive buyers, low switching costs
- Two paths: (a) control cost drivers in current value chain, (b) reengineer value chain

**9 Cost Drivers:** Economies of scale, learning curve, input costs, value chain linkages, shared facilities, vertical integration level, first-mover advantage, capacity utilization, policy choices

**2. Broad Differentiation:**
- Incorporate attributes buyers value and will pay premium for
- Success enables: premium pricing, increased unit sales, buyer loyalty
- Works when: diverse buyer preferences, few rivals pursuing similar approach, rapid innovation cycles
- Fails when: quick competitor copying, "so what" reception, overspending on differentiation

**3. Best-Cost Provider (Hybrid):**
- Match rivals on quality/features + beat them on cost
- "More value for the money"
- Works when: buyer diversity + price sensitivity coexist

**4. Focused Low-Cost:** Serve niche buyers at lower cost than broad competitors

**5. Focused Differentiation:** Serve niche buyers with specialized, appealing offering

#### Chapter 8: Corporate Strategy — Diversification and the Multibusiness Company

**When to Diversify:** Single-business company faces diminishing market opportunities and stagnating sales

**Purpose:** Build shareholder value — achieve **1+1=3** performance benefits

**Three Tests for Diversification:**
1. **Attractiveness test** — Is the industry attractive?
2. **Cost-of-entry test** — Is the acquisition price reasonable?
3. **Better-off test** — Will the combined entity be stronger?

**Entry Modes:** Acquisition (most frequent), internal startup (slowest), joint venture (least durable)

**Two Approaches:**
| Related Diversification | Unrelated Diversification |
|------------------------|--------------------------|
| Strategic fits across value chains | Any profitable business on good terms |
| Transfer skills, share activities | Spread risk across industries |
| Leverage brand name | Financial gain from undervalued assets |
| Build competitive advantage | Risk: too many businesses = management complexity |

**6-Step Evaluation Process:**
1. Evaluate long-term attractiveness of each industry
2. Evaluate competitive strength of each business unit
3. Check for cross-business strategic fits
4. Check whether resources fit requirements (cash cows vs. cash hogs)
5. Rank performance prospects → allocate resources
6. Craft new strategic moves (acquire, divest, restructure, globalize)

**5 Post-Diversification Paths:** Stick with lineup, diversify further, retrench (divest), restructure (divest + acquire), multinational diversification

### Section D: Executing the Strategy

> *"Whereas crafting strategy is largely a market-driven activity, executing strategy is primarily an operations activity revolving around management of people and business processes."*

#### Chapter 10: Building an Organization Capable of Good Strategy Execution
- Staffing the organization
- Building core competencies and competitive capabilities
- Matching organizational structure to strategy
- People → Capabilities → Structure alignment

#### Chapter 11: Managing Internal Operations
- Actions that promote good strategy execution:
  - Allocating resources to strategy-critical activities
  - Establishing strategy-supportive policies and procedures
  - Continuous improvement (TQM, Six Sigma, BPR)
  - Installing information and operating systems
  - Tying rewards to achievement of strategic targets

#### Chapter 12: Corporate Culture and Leadership
- Shaping a strategy-supportive corporate culture
- Leading the strategy execution process
- 4 things successful managers do:
  1. **Stay on top of things** — visible, listening, coaching, cheerleading
  2. **Put constructive pressure** on the organization
  3. **Keep focus on operating excellence** — champion innovation, best practices
  4. **Exert clout** in developing competencies and capabilities

## CORE THEME OF THE BOOK

> **"Whether a company wins or loses in the marketplace is directly attributable to the potential of that company's strategy and the zeal and controls with which the strategy is executed."**

The book's central argument: Strategy is not just about having a brilliant plan. It's about:
1. **Analyzing** the external and internal environment rigorously
2. **Crafting** a strategy that fits those realities and builds competitive advantage
3. **Executing** with operational excellence and adaptive leadership
4. **Adjusting** continuously based on results and changing conditions

The strategy-making/executing process is the *core management function* — everything else (marketing, finance, operations) serves it.
