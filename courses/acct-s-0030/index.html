📋 Official Description: Accounting conventions; developing abilities to evaluate and use accounting data; the mechanics of financial accounting and the overall effect of alternative accounting procedures on published financial reports; the linkage between accounting information and management planning, decision making and control.

🔷 Key Frameworks

Core mental models and methodologies for this course:

The Accounting Equation

Assets = Liabilities + Owners' Equity. The fundamental principle of double-entry bookkeeping. Every transaction affects at least two accounts — the equation must always balance. Foundation of all financial statements.

Accrual vs. Cash Accounting

Accrual: revenue/expenses recognized when earned/incurred, regardless of cash flow. Cash: recognized only when money changes hands. GAAP and IFRS both require accrual basis for public companies.

The Four Core Financial Statements

1. Balance Sheet — financial position at a point in time. 2. Income Statement — profitability over a period. 3. Statement of Cash Flows — operating, investing, financing activities. 4. Statement of Changes in Equity — retained earnings, dividends, other comprehensive income.

Alternative Accounting Procedures

Inventory methods (FIFO vs LIFO vs Weighted Average), Depreciation (Straight-line vs Accelerated), Revenue recognition timing. These choices materially affect reported earnings, tax liability, and key ratios — understanding the impact is critical for executives.

GAAP vs IFRS vs TFRS

US GAAP (rule-based, SEC/FASB) vs IFRS (principle-based, IASB). Thailand adopted TFRS (aligned with IFRS). Critical for reading financials across borders. Differences in inventory (LIFO banned under IFRS), R&D capitalization, and lease accounting.

Financial Ratio Analysis

Liquidity (current, quick ratios), Solvency (debt-to-equity, interest coverage), Profitability (ROE, ROA, gross/net margins), Efficiency (inventory turnover, AR days, asset turnover). Ratios are only meaningful in comparison — across time, competitors, or industry benchmarks.

🏢 Case Studies

Enron (2001) — The Definitive Accounting Fraud
Off-balance-sheet entities (SPEs), mark-to-market abuse, auditor failure. Led directly to Sarbanes-Oxley Act and dissolution of Arthur Andersen.
WorldCom (2002) — Capitalization Fraud
$3.8 billion of operating expenses capitalized as assets. Classic example of how accounting choices distort the true picture.
Tesla — Revenue Recognition Complexity
Deferred revenue, regulatory credits, full self-driving recognition. Real-world complexity of applying accounting rules to innovative business models.
